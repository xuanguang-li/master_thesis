\begin{abstract}
This paper asks how much an investment demand shock contributes to the U.S. business cycle once the investment-specific technology (IST) shock is also present, and how the two differ in their effects. Both shocks move investment, but differ in origin: the IST shock raises the technology that converts consumption goods into investment goods, whereas the investment demand shock, following \cite{nireiOriginsPropagationAnimal}, traces back to idiosyncratic productivity shocks under lumpy investment and appears in the aggregate as a deviation of investment from the decision households would otherwise have made. I represent it through a two-step solution that fixes the endogenous investment decision and then adds the exogenous shift, embed this in a DSGE model with nominal rigidities, and estimate it on U.S. data using Bayesian methods. In the baseline the shock explains roughly 40\% of the variance of output and 50\% of investment, comparable to the IST shock, and generates an inflationary expansion through a wealth channel that raises hours, wages, and output together. These figures should be read with caution: they shrink sharply at business-cycle frequencies and in larger models, and identification is indirect. The results suggest the investment demand shock can play a quantitatively meaningful role alongside the IST shock and acts on the business cycle, but that this conclusion depends materially on specification and a weakly identified shock.
\end{abstract}
