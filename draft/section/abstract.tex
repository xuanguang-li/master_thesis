\begin{abstract}
This paper studies the role of investment demand shock in the U.S. business cycle, as an alternative to the classic investment specific technology (IST) shock. Following the idea of \cite{nireiOriginsPropagationAnimal}, I introduce an investment demand process that shifts investment exogenously after the baseline endogenous investment decision is formed, which requires a two-step equilibrium solution. I build an estimated DSGE model with nominal rigidities, investment adjustment costs, and endogenous capital utilization, and estimate it using Bayesian methods with U.S. data.

The estimation results show that investment demand shock is a competitive driving force of the business cycle. In the baseline model, investment demand shock alone explains about 40\% of the forecast error variance of output and about 50\% of investment, and it generates an inflationary expansion that contrasts with the deflationary effect of IST shock. Robustness tests using the relative price of investment to discipline IST shock, and using a larger Smets--Wouters type model, both support the view that investment demand shock remains quantitatively important.
\end{abstract}
